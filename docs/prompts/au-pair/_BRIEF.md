## Professional regulatory bodies

Au pair work has **no professional regulator** in any destination — it is a cultural exchange, not a skilled profession. The relevant bodies regulate the **sponsor agencies and host families**, not the au pair's qualifications.

| Country | Regulator (of sponsor agencies / host families) | SA-side touchpoint |
|---|---|---|
| **USA** | US Department of State, Bureau of Educational and Cultural Affairs (ECA) — designates and audits sponsor agencies under 22 CFR 62.31 | DIRCO (apostille of PCC + medical + reference letters); SAPS (Police Clearance Certificate); US Embassy Pretoria / Johannesburg / Cape Town / Durban consulates for J-1 visa interview |
| **Netherlands** | IND (Immigratie- en Naturalisatiedienst) — maintains the Public Register of Recognised Sponsors; only au pair agencies on this list can apply for au-pair residence permits | DIRCO (PCC apostille); Dutch Embassy Pretoria for MVV (provisional residence permit) interview |
| **Germany** | Bundesagentur für Arbeit (BA) — operates the "Au-pair bei deutschen Familien" RAL Gütezeichen quality-mark for agencies; BAMF for residence permit policy | DIRCO (PCC apostille); German Mission in SA (Pretoria/Cape Town/Durban/Johannesburg) for au-pair visa; Goethe-Institut Johannesburg/Pretoria for A1 German exam |
| **France** | Préfecture / OFII — registration on arrival; the host family must register the contract at DREETS | DIRCO (apostille); French Consulate Cape Town/Johannesburg via VFS Global; Alliance Française for French A1 prep |

**SA bodies:**
- **SAPS** — Police Clearance Certificate (R150, ~6 weeks turnaround)
- **DIRCO** — Apostille service for PCC, medical certificate, reference letters, degree (if any)
- **SAQA** — Optional: NOT required for au pair work but useful if applying with a relevant qualification (childcare, nursing assistant)
- **No SACE / no professional body** — au pair work is not formal teaching or care work

## Known scam patterns

Au-pair scams targeting SA candidates fall into 5 distinct patterns. List **all 5** with specifics in the scam vault:

1. **Upfront-fee recruiters who don't deliver placement.** Legitimate USA J-1 sponsors charge a program fee (typically USD $1,000–1,800) but ONLY after match confirmation, and they all pay return flight + insurance + program academy. Scam recruiters demand R5,000–R30,000 "registration fees" via WhatsApp, then disappear or simply forward CVs to AuPairWorld with no sponsor relationship.

2. **"Direct host-family" placements that bypass the J-1 sponsor.** Illegal under 22 CFR 62.31. The au pair arrives on a B-2 tourist visa or B-1 business visa and works without authorisation — this is visa fraud, deportable, and the candidate has no legal recourse against an abusive host family.

3. **Netherlands "private au-pair" arrangements that bypass IND recognition.** Illegal under Dutch immigration law. Au pair has no residence permit, no work authorisation, and cannot register at Gemeente.

4. **"Guaranteed placement" agencies charging R10,000–R30,000 upfront.** No legitimate program guarantees a match — Cultural Care, AIFS, OVC all charge only at match confirmation or even later. Pressure tactics + WhatsApp + bank deposit before sponsor letter = scam.

5. **Fake host families on AuPairWorld / GreatAuPair.** Catfish profiles harvest passport copies and personal details, or ask the au pair to send "flight booking" money via Western Union. Legitimate families never ask the au pair to pay anything; sponsor agencies arrange flights.

Plus context the scam vault should also surface:
- **The "passport-holding" host-family abuse pattern** (host keeps the au pair's passport on arrival — illegal in USA/NL/DE; reportable to sponsor's emergency line, the US State Dept Office of Designation, or the IND)
- **Trafficking risk** in unstructured (non-program) au-pair arrangements, especially in Gulf states (which are NOT covered by this guide — au-pair work in UAE/Saudi falls under domestic-worker labour law, not exchange-visitor visas, and is a different and much higher-risk product)

## Legitimate programmes / bilateral agreements

- **US-State-Department-designated J-1 sponsor list** — public register at https://j1visa.state.gov/participants/how-to-apply/sponsor-search/. ~12 designated au-pair sponsors. South Africans should apply only through this list.
- **IND Public Register of Recognised Sponsors** — public register at https://ind.nl/en/public-register-recognised-sponsors. Select "Exchange (Uitwisseling)" category for au-pair-eligible agencies.
- **BA Gütezeichen for German au-pair agencies** — RAL quality mark, published at https://www.guetegemeinschaft-aupair.de/
- **No bilateral SA–destination au-pair agreement** exists for any country. All routes are unilateral programs of the destination country, not SA-government-negotiated agreements.

- **A1 German exam** Goethe-Institut Johannesburg pricing — quote May 2026 rate

**Confirmed facts the vault prompts must NOT re-debate:**
- USA J-1 Au Pair age range is **18–26** (must be under 27 at visa issuance)
- Maximum USA program length is **24 months total** (12-month initial + 12-month extension)
- Netherlands age range is **18–25** (must be under 26)
- Germany age range is **18–26** (up to 29 if entering on Working Holiday — but SA has NO Working Holiday agreement with Germany, so the SA cap is firmly 18–26)
- France age range is **18–30**
- UK does NOT have an au-pair visa — do not list UK as an au-pair destination

**Audience guardrails:**
- This product targets **SA women aged 18–26 from middle-income households** considering au-pair work to fund their gap year and gain US/EU work experience. Some candidates are family breadwinners helping support younger siblings — be sensitive to cost-of-living and remittance considerations.
- Most candidates do NOT have a degree (this is intentional — au-pair is the no-degree route). Avoid prompts that assume tertiary qualifications.
- Driver's licence is the single biggest practical filter for the USA. Flag this upfront in the destination vault.

**Out-of-scope (do NOT cover in this guide):**
- Gulf state "au-pair" arrangements (Saudi, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait) — these are domestic-worker visas, not au-pair exchanges, and carry significantly higher abuse and kafala-system risks. Separate (and currently unbuilt) product territory.
- Working Holiday Visas (UK YMS, Canada IEC, Australia WHV) — different product (covered by jobabroad seasonal/general routes if at all).
- Domestic-worker visas anywhere — these are NOT cultural exchange and have completely different legal frameworks.
- Au-pair-to-PR pathways — au-pair visas do NOT lead to permanent residence in any destination. The product must be honest that this is a 12–24 month experience, not an emigration route. Candidates seeking PR should be referred to the relevant skilled-worker pathway (nursing, teaching, IT) instead.
